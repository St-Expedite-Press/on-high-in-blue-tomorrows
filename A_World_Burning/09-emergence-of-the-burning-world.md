> _A counterfactual climatology imposed on a canonical natural-historical image system._

You are not:

- stressing
    
- warping
    
- heating
    
- bleaching
    
- choking

## Classes of appearance / coloration manipulation you can now do

I’ll group these by _conceptual force_, not by tool.

---

### 1. Thermal shift (heat as a global condition)

You can remap color in ways that simulate **temperature stress** rather than mood.

Examples:

- compress greens toward sickly yellows
    
- drive shadows toward ember reds or bruise purples
    
- collapse cool blues into ash-grays
    
- exaggerate infrared-adjacent channels
    

Because you have scalar measures (luminance, entropy, contrast), you can:

- apply heat more strongly to already stressed regions
    
- leave high-contrast anatomy intact while backgrounds scorch
    
- make “life” and “environment” respond differently
    

This produces **uneven burning**, which reads as ecological.

---

### 2. Night artifacts (non-human optics)

This is where it gets interesting.

You can introduce:

- low-light noise patterns
    
- color channel misalignment
    
- bloom and haloing around edges
    
- desaturation except in biologically salient regions (eyes, beaks)
    

This mimics:

- firelight
    
- moonlight
    
- infrared
    
- degraded human vision
    

The key is: these are not stylistic effects, they are **sensor conditions**.  
You are asking: _what does Audubon look like when seen by damaged instruments in a damaged world?_

---

### 3. Atmospheric interference (smoke, dust, haze)

Using segmentation, you can:

- treat background differently from organism
    
- flatten depth cues
    
- introduce chromatic veiling
    
- reduce clarity without erasing detail
    

This creates:

- a sense of distance collapse
    
- a choking of space
    
- an image that feels _unbreathable_
    

The plate still depicts nature—but nature is no longer legible in the old way.

---

### 4. Selective vitality (life against collapse)

Because you can isolate organisms:

- you can preserve color saturation in plumage
    
- while letting surroundings bleach or burn
    
- or invert it: let the bird itself look irradiated, overexposed, unstable
    

This lets you play with:

- extinction narratives
    
- last-specimen aesthetics
    
- the idea of life persisting in hostile chromatic fields
    

This is where _The Burning World_ becomes emotionally legible.

---

### 5. Degradation as time, not damage

Instead of “ruining” images, you can:

- introduce slow chromatic drift
    
- banding
    
- uneven fading
    
- color collapse in one channel only
    

These read as:

- chemical instability
    
- archival failure
    
- time accelerated
    

Audubon becomes something like a fossil record under stress.

## Why this is stronger than “climate-themed art”

Because you are not illustrating climate change.

- taxonomic

- clarity becomes impossible
    
- color no longer stabilizes meaning
    
- representation itself begins to fail
